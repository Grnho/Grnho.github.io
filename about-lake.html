About Lake Mendota

Lake Mendota is the largest and northernmost of the four lakes in Madison, Wisconsin. It covers approximately 9,781 acres and is a central feature of the University of Wisconsin-Madison campus. The lake provides countless opportunities for recreation, research, and appreciation of natural beauty.

The Memorial Union Terrace offers one of the best vantage points to view the lake, especially during sunset when the sky and water create spectacular color combinations.

Why the Sky Turns Pink & Blue

Have you ever wondered why sunsets look so beautiful over Lake Mendota? The answer lies in the science of light and atmosphere. When sunlight travels through the Earth's atmosphere, it interacts with tiny particles that change how we see different colors.

Rayleigh Scattering
This phenomenon occurs when light hits tiny particles in the atmosphere. Shorter wavelengths of light (like blue and violet) scatter more easily, which is why the sky appears blue during the day. However, during sunset, sunlight travels through more atmosphere, allowing longer wavelengths (like red, orange, and pink) to reach our eyes.
Golden Hour
This magical time occurs about an hour before sunset (and after sunrise). The sun is low on the horizon, creating soft, warm light with beautiful shadows and minimal harsh contrasts. It's called golden hour because everything appears bathed in a warm, golden glow.
Water Reflections
Lake Mendota acts like a mirror, reflecting the colorful sky above. When the water is calm, you get perfect mirror images of the sunset, doubling the visual impact of the colors.
Atmospheric Conditions
Particles in the air, such as dust, moisture, and pollution, can enhance or change the colors we see during sunset. This is why some sunsets are more vivid than others - it depends on what's in the air.

Understanding these scientific concepts helps us appreciate the beauty of nature even more. Next time you see a spectacular sunset, you'll know the science behind those amazing colors!
